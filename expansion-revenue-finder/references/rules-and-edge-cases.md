# Behavioral Rules and Edge Cases

## Behavioral Rules

1. Ground every opportunity in evidence. "They might want more seats" is not an opportunity. "They have 47 of 50 licensed seats active, added 12 users last quarter, and grew headcount 30% this year" is an opportunity.

2. Do not confuse activation with expansion. If a customer is not using features they already have, the play is enablement, not upsell. Flag these as "activation opportunities" separately. Selling more to a customer who is not using what they have destroys trust.

3. Respect account health. Do not recommend expansion plays for accounts at risk of churn. If the relationship is unhealthy, the first priority is stabilization. Flag these and note expansion should wait until health improves.

4. Think in customer value, not quota. Frame every recommendation around the customer's outcome, not the revenue captured. "This gives their marketing team the analytics module, saving the 10 hours per week they spend in spreadsheets" beats "This is a $30K upsell."

5. Be precise about timing. Tie recommendations to natural buying moments: renewals, budget cycles, headcount planning, new fiscal years, strategic planning periods. An opportunity with the right timing is worth 3x one with bad timing.

6. Quantify everything. Estimated ARR impact for every opportunity. Total expansion revenue identified. Percentage of portfolio with expansion potential. Size the opportunity in dollars.

7. Differentiate organic growth from active expansion. Some expansion happens naturally (usage-based billing grows with usage). Separate organic growth from opportunities requiring active sales or CS effort. Both matter but require different actions.

8. Stack-rank ruthlessly. A 50-account portfolio might yield 200 opportunities. The team cannot pursue all of them. The scoring and tiering system forces prioritization. Do not soften it by putting everything in Tier 2.

## Edge Cases

- **Very small portfolio (1-3 accounts)**: Go deeper on each account. Add more detail to pitch and approach. Include alternative expansion paths if the primary opportunity does not convert.

- **No usage data available**: Work from relationship signals, company growth data, and contract terms. Score with lower confidence and recommend instrumentation improvements. Org growth, competitive displacement, and contract structure can still surface opportunities.

- **Customer just renewed**: Expansion conversations are most natural 2-4 months after renewal once the customer has settled in. Flag timing as Tier 2 or Tier 3 unless a strong trigger exists (usage ceiling, new department request).

- **Free or trial accounts**: These are conversion opportunities, not expansion. Note them separately as "Conversion Candidates" with a likelihood-of-converting-to-paid scoring emphasis.

- **Multi-product customers**: Analyze each product line separately, then look for cross-sell between products. The most valuable expansion is often getting a single-product customer to adopt a second product.

- **Channel/partner-managed accounts**: Visibility into usage and relationship health may be limited. Recommend partner enablement programs and joint business reviews to surface opportunities.
